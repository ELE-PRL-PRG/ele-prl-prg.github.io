Jan Vitek
j.vitek@neu.edu
http://janvitek.org
Joined Czech Technical University, 2016
Joined Northeastern, 2014
Joined Purdue, 1999
PhD, University of Geneva, 1999
MSc, University of Victoria, 1995

My name is Jan Vitek, and I am a Professor of Computer Science at Northeastern University.

My research focus on design and implementation of programming abstractions in real-time embedded systems, concurrent and distributed systems and more recently scalable data analytics. I have published in venues that specialize in Programming Languages, Virtual Machines, Compilers, Software Engineering, Real-time Computing, and Bioinformatics. I enjoy beautiful code that solves real-world problems.

Together with Noble and Potter, we proposed a concept that became known as Ownership Types, and I was one of the designers of the Thorn language. My current projects look at gaining a better understanding of the JavaScript language and supporting scalable data analysis in R.

Tomáš Kalibera
tomas.kalibera@r-project.org
Joined Czech Technical University, 2016
Post-doc at University of Kent, 2010-2012
Post-doc at Purdue, 2007-2009
Joined Charles University, 2007
PhD, Charles University, 2006

My name is Tomas Kalibera and I work on the R language. I first encountered it while learning statistics during my doctoral studies. My dissertation was about statistical methods in regression benchmarking. At that time, R was a bit slow (and still is), but extremely useful and growing in popularity. During my postdoc at Purdue University in the USA. I worked on Ovm, a real-time Java virtual machine, where I enjoyed low-level programming of the real-time garbage collector, interrupt handling, or support for embedded OS.

Finally, I switched teams, and is now working on R itself. Making R better is a unique challenge, and one that I am enjoying. R has a massive codebase of applications that use every corner case of the language. The VM For R can be changed as long as "it makes sense" and it does not break applications. This is completely different from building a language ecosystem from scratch or from improving an implementation of a language that has a specification.

Filip Křikava
Post-doctoral researcher
f.krikava@neu.edu
http://fikovnik.net
Joined Czech Technical University, 2015
Joined INRIA Lille, 2013
PhD, University of Nice Sophia-Antipolis, 2013

My name is Filip Krikava. I am a member of the ELE team currently exiled in Boston. I did my doctoral studies at the French national centre of scientific research (CNRS) in the I3S laboratory in Nice Sophia-Antipolis. I did a postdoc at INRIA in the Spirals Team. My primary research interests were in self-adaptive software systems. These systems are characterized by the ability to continuously operate under varying runtime conditions. I also worked on domain-specific languages for consistency checking and transformation of software models.

Before working as a researcher, I worked as a software engineer in many different parts of the world. After leaving the Czech Republic for more than a decade, I’ve finally came back and decided to joined FIT in the software engineering department ,and later the PRL group. Currently, I'm interested in various aspects of programming languages in particular in static and dynamic program analysis. Most recently I have been working on dynamic tracing in R, but I look forward for program analysis of modeling languages.

Paley Li
pa.li@neu.edu

My name is Paley Li. I come from Wellington, New Zealand, where I did both my undergraduate and doctoral studies at Victoria University.

My passion for computing developed once late. Growing up I had no appreciation for what a computer could do, but after writing my first program , I was completely mesmerised and switched to study computer science. Throughout my studies I was enamored by the expressive power of the computer languages I used. I viewed them no differently to the many natural languages used all over the world. The possibility for ambiguity and the way a language would dictate how you thought about a problem lead me to a PhD in this field. After earning my doctorate, I joined the Programming Research Lab at Northeastern University, where I was able to work alongside amazing researchers. I was able to learn many topics that were previously unknown to me, such as virtual machines, and just-in compilers.

I am excited and enthusiastic to have the opportunity join FIT. I look forward to being able contribute my expertise to the ELE project.

Konrad Siek
Post-doctoral researcher
siekkonr@fit.cvut.cz
Joined Czech Technical University, 2017
PhD, Poznań University of Technology, 2017

My name is Konrad Siek. I am a new member of the ELE team.

I defended my PhD thesis at the Distributed Systems Research Group at Poznań University of Technology at the beginning of January 2017. My research focus were distributed pessimistic transactional memory systems and their safety properties. While working on TM, I developed a passion for making concurrency easier for application programmers, which requires automatization and transparency. This naturally led me to get interested in programming language research.

I joined the PRL group in January 2017, where I am looking forward to working in programming language research. Since the field is new to me, everything is alien and fascinating, and I have a lot to learn. I am currently discovering the secrets of compilation, static analysis, and I plan to work on the R language. In the long term I hope to be able to merge my penchant for concurrency and my new-found relish for programming languages into a contribution to either field.

I was always interested in programming, but before I embarked on a research career in CS, I got a B.A. in English philology and taught English. I also plied my trade as a software developer for a year.
